What are the Best Ways to Learn?: Study Smarter, not Harder

Do you ever feel like your study habits aren’t working anymore? Are you wondering how to do better in class and on exams? Many students find that the way they studied in high school doesn’t work as well in college—and that’s normal. College is different: professors are less involved, classes are larger and harder, exams have more weight, and there’s a lot more reading to do. This doesn’t mean you’re doing something wrong—it just means you need new, more effective study strategies.

This guide provides useful tips for better studying. By adding these strategies to your routine, you can learn more efficiently and perform better in your courses. Try different methods and see which ones suit you best.

  1. Studying Doesn’t Mean Just Reading

    2. Simply reading and re-reading your notes or textbooks may feel productive, but it is actually one of the least effective study strategies. This passive approach does not actively engage your brain with the material. When students only “do the readings” for class without further interaction—such as summarizing, questioning, or applying what they learned—they are not truly studying. Instead, they are merely reviewing information on the surface level, which often leads to quick forgetting (Brown, Roediger, & McDaniel, 2014).

    Active studying involves deeper cognitive engagement, such as self-testing, explaining concepts in your own words, or connecting new information to what you already know. According to Dunlosky et al. (2013), strategies like self-quizzing and spaced practice are far more effective than simple re-reading because they help strengthen memory and improve long-term retention. Therefore, students should replace passive reading with active learning techniques that require thought, retrieval, and application.

  2. Understand the Study Cycle

    3. The Study Cycle, created by Frank Christ, divides studying into five key steps: previewing, attending class, reviewing, studying, and checking your understanding. Each stage supports effective learning, but students often skip steps and lose valuable opportunities to strengthen memory (Christ, 2010).

    For example, skipping readings before class might seem harmless, but it prevents students from learning through both reading and listening, which improves understanding. Previewing helps prepare the mind, while reviewing and studying reinforce what was learned. Finally, checking your understanding through self-testing helps ensure mastery (Brown, Roediger, & McDaniel, 2014). Following all steps of the Study Cycle helps students learn more efficiently and retain information longer

  3. Spacing out is good

    4. A highly effective study method is distributed practice, which means spreading study sessions across several short periods over days or weeks rather than cramming in one sitting. This method helps students learn more deeply and retain information longer (Newport, 2007). Studying a little each day for every subject is more effective than long sessions, which often lead to fatigue and poor concentration.

    Creating a daily task list can help students manage their time and include short, active study periods for each class. For example, working on a few math problems daily or reviewing notes for 15–20 minutes can maintain focus and prevent last-minute cramming. Spaced study also reduces procrastination since shorter, regular sessions feel less overwhelming. For memorization, reviewing flashcards several times throughout the day is more effective than one long study period (Wissman & Rawson, 2012).

  4. It’s good to be intense

    5. Not all study methods are equally effective. You can achieve more by studying intensively, which means focusing for shorter, highly concentrated periods rather than studying for long, unfocused stretches. Research shows that distributed and intensive study sessions—lasting about 30 to 45 minutes—are more productive than extended sessions that often lead to fatigue and distraction (Newport, 2007).

    Active learning strategies, such as self-testing, help make study sessions more engaging and efficient (Brown, Roediger, & McDaniel, 2014). However, studying for hours without breaks tends to reduce attention and motivation. Instead, setting a goal to quiz yourself for around 45 minutes, followed by a short break, helps maintain focus and improves long-term retention. Short, focused study periods also create a sense of urgency, which can reduce procrastination and enhance productivity.

  5. Silence Isn’t Always Golden

    6. When studying, it’s important to find the environment that helps you focus best. While some people prefer quiet spaces like libraries, others may concentrate better with a bit of background noise or soft music. Research shows that the ideal study environment varies by individual—some students perform better with low-level background sounds or instrumental music, while others find such noise distracting (Perham & Currie, 2014). If complete silence feels uncomfortable, consider studying in areas with mild ambient noise, such as a library’s first floor or a café.

    Also, remember that active studying is often not silent—it involves activities like reading aloud, explaining concepts, or discussing ideas, all of which can strengthen learning (McGuire, 2015).

  6. Problems Are Your Friend

    7. For technical subjects such as mathematics or economics, regularly working through and reworking problems is one of the most effective ways to learn. It’s essential not only to solve problems but also to understand and explain each step and why it works. According to Newport (2007), practicing problems is often more beneficial than simply reading the textbook.

    During lectures, students should take detailed notes of sample problems demonstrated by the instructor, annotating each step and clarifying any confusing parts. Even if some steps are missed, recording the problem and solution can still be useful for later review. When studying for exams, compiling a list of problems from class materials and practice exercises—and then solving them while explaining the reasoning—helps reinforce understanding and improve problem-solving skills (Carrier, 2003).

  7. Rethink the habit of multitasking

    8. Many studies show that it actually reduces productivity and leads to poorer outcomes (Junco, 2012).

    To study more efficiently, it’s important to remove distractions while you work. Activities like checking social media, browsing the web, gaming, or texting can greatly lower your focus and the effectiveness of your study time. Evidence shows that multitasking, such as replying to messages while trying to study, not only extends the time required to learn new information but also diminishes the quality of what you learn (Junco, 2012).

    By cutting out these distractions, you can concentrate fully on your studies. If your coursework doesn’t require a computer, avoid using it. You can also install apps that help restrict access to certain websites, turn off your phone, and reward yourself with short, timed social media breaks after focused study periods. For more suggestions, check out resources on managing technology effectively.

  8. Change up your study environment from time to time

    9. Explore multiple study areas on and around campus so you can switch locations whenever your current space no longer helps you stay productive.

    It’s also important to recognize when and where you focus best. For instance, your concentration at 10:00 PM might not be as strong as it is at 10:00 AM. Some students may work better in a café with light background noise, while others prefer the quiet of a residence hall study lounge. Studying in bed, however, might make you too relaxed or even cause you to fall asleep.

    Having a range of study locations ensures that no matter where you are, you can find an environment that supports your learning. If one spot becomes too comfortable or distracting, it’s a good idea to switch to a new setting to maintain productivity.

  9. Become a teacher

    10. One of the best ways to master new material is to teach it as if you were the instructor. Try explaining the concepts in your own words—either to a study partner, a group, or even just to yourself. Speaking the material out loud helps you identify gaps in your understanding and strengthens your memory of the content. While explaining, use real-life examples and draw connections between ideas, just like a teacher would. It’s perfectly fine to refer to your notes at first, but aim to reach the point where you can teach the material without them.

    Another effective method is to create your own quizzes. This helps you think from your instructor’s perspective—what key information might they test? Self-quizzing is a powerful study strategy that enhances recall. Make a study guide containing questions and answers and review it periodically throughout the day. Focus on the questions you struggle with and practice saying your answers aloud. For more technical subjects, work through practice problems and explain each step from start to finish. Redo any problems that are challenging. This active approach to learning engages your brain deeply and significantly improves retention (Craik, 1975).

  10. Use downtime to your advantage

    11. Make good use of your downtime, especially during lighter academic weeks. These “easy” weeks often come before a busy period, so take advantage of them to get ahead on assignments or begin long-term projects and papers. Use any extra time to stay productive rather than waiting until deadlines approach.

    Aim to dedicate time to every class each week, even when there are no immediate tasks due. Ideally, you should spend a bit of time on each subject daily—around 30 minutes per class. This adds up to roughly three hours per week per course, but spacing your study time across several days is far more effective than trying to fit it all into one long session. If you’ve already finished your required work for a class, use that time to review material, prepare for upcoming lessons, or make progress on larger projects.
